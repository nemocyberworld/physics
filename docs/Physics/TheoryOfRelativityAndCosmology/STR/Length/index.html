Special Theory of Relativity

Chapter: Length Contraction, Time Dilation & Velocity Addition

Overview

The Special Theory of Relativity, developed by Albert Einstein in 1905, revolutionized our understanding of space and time. It introduced groundbreaking ideas such as the constancy of the speed of light, and that the laws of physics are the same in all inertial frames. In this lesson, we will explore:

  • Length Contraction
  • Time Dilation
  • Velocity Addition Theorem
Fun Fact: GPS satellites use Einstein's relativity equations to stay accurate. Without them, GPS readings would drift by kilometers every day!

1. Length Contraction

When an object moves close to the speed of light, it appears contracted in the direction of motion from the viewpoint of a stationary observer. The formula for length contraction is:

$$ L = L_0 \sqrt{1 - \frac{v^2}{c^2}} $$

Where:

  • L is the observed length
  • L0 is the proper length (rest frame)
  • v is the object's velocity
  • c is the speed of light

2. Time Dilation

Moving clocks tick slower from the perspective of a stationary observer. The formula for time dilation is:

$$ t = \frac{t_0}{\sqrt{1 - \frac{v^2}{c^2}}} $$

Where:

  • t is the dilated time
  • t0 is the proper time
  • v is the velocity
  • c is the speed of light

3. Velocity Addition Theorem

When combining velocities close to the speed of light, we cannot use classical addition. Instead, we use:

$$ u' = \frac{u + v}{1 + \frac{uv}{c^2}} $$

This ensures no object exceeds the speed of light.

Real-World Applications

  • GPS Technology: Must account for time dilation due to both special and general relativity.
  • Particle Accelerators: Electrons and protons exhibit relativistic mass changes and time dilation.
  • Astrophysics: Predicting star behavior and light from fast-moving objects (quasars, pulsars).

More Fun Facts

  • At 0.99c, time passes roughly 7 times slower than at rest!
  • Muons created in the upper atmosphere reach Earth because of time dilation – without it, they’d decay too quickly.
