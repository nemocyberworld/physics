Cosmology: Rotating Black Holes (Kerr Black Holes)

This lesson explores the fascinating world of rotating black holes, also known as Kerr black holes. Unlike the simpler Schwarzschild black holes, Kerr black holes spin, giving rise to rich and complex physics with many real-world implications.

Click the section headings to expand detailed explanations, examples, equations, and fun facts.

What is a Rotating Black Hole?

Kerr black holes were discovered by Roy Kerr in 1963 as a solution to Einstein's field equations that describes a rotating, uncharged black hole. Rotation means the black hole has angular momentum, profoundly affecting spacetime around it.

Unlike Schwarzschild black holes, Kerr black holes have:

  • An ergosphere — a region outside the event horizon where spacetime is dragged around the black hole.
  • A ring-shaped singularity instead of a point.
  • Two horizons: an outer event horizon and an inner Cauchy horizon.

The rotation causes effects like frame dragging, which means that anything close to the black hole is forced to rotate along with it.

The Kerr Metric

The Kerr solution to Einstein's equations is expressed via a complicated metric, describing how distances and time behave near the rotating black hole. A simplified form of the Kerr metric includes the parameters M (mass) and a (the spin parameter), where:

$$ a = \frac{J}{Mc} $$
J is the angular momentum, M the mass, and c the speed of light.

The radius of the outer event horizon is:

$$ r_+ = \frac{GM}{c^2} + \sqrt{\left(\frac{GM}{c^2}\right)^2 - a^2} $$

Notice that if the spin \(a\) is zero, this reduces to the Schwarzschild radius.

Suppose a black hole has mass \(M = 10 M_\odot\) (10 times the Sun's mass), and angular momentum \(J = 0.8 \, \frac{GM^2}{c}\).

Calculate \(a\):

$$ a = \frac{J}{M c} = 0.8 \frac{GM}{c^2} $$

Then the outer horizon radius is:

$$ r_+ = \frac{GM}{c^2} + \sqrt{\left(\frac{GM}{c^2}\right)^2 - (0.8 \frac{GM}{c^2})^2} = \frac{GM}{c^2} (1 + \sqrt{1 - 0.64}) $$

Which evaluates to about \(1.6 \times \frac{GM}{c^2}\).

Ergosphere and Frame Dragging

One of the most intriguing features of Kerr black holes is the ergosphere, a region outside the event horizon where spacetime itself is dragged around by the black hole’s rotation.

Inside the ergosphere, it’s impossible to stand still relative to distant stars — everything is forced to move in the direction of rotation.

The boundary of the ergosphere (the static limit) lies outside the event horizon and has radius:

$$ r_{erg}(\theta) = \frac{GM}{c^2} + \sqrt{\left(\frac{GM}{c^2}\right)^2 - a^2 \cos^2 \theta} $$

Here, \(\theta\) is the polar angle — the ergosphere’s shape is oblate, bulging at the equator.

This region allows the famous Penrose process, where particles can gain energy by extracting rotational energy from the black hole.

Imagine a particle enters the ergosphere and splits into two: one falls into the black hole, the other escapes with more energy than the original particle, effectively extracting rotational energy.

This theoretical process shows that black holes can spin down by losing angular momentum.

Real-World Examples and Applications

Many astrophysical black holes are believed to be Kerr black holes, as stars naturally have angular momentum before collapsing.

  • Active Galactic Nuclei (AGN): Supermassive Kerr black holes power quasars, emitting vast jets and radiation.
  • Accretion Disks: The frame dragging affects the inner edge of disks, influencing X-ray emissions from binary systems.
  • Gravitational Waves: Merging Kerr black holes emit gravitational waves detected by LIGO/Virgo.
Fun Fact: The spin of black holes can be measured by observing the X-ray spectra of matter swirling near the event horizon!

Summary

Rotating black holes introduce rich, fascinating physics beyond static solutions. The Kerr solution describes how angular momentum changes spacetime structure, creating ergospheres, frame dragging, and multiple horizons.

These properties are essential for understanding black hole energy extraction, astrophysical jet formation, and the signals we observe from black hole mergers.
