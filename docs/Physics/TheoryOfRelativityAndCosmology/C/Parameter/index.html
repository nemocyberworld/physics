Cosmology Lesson: Cosmological Parameters

Cosmological parameters are fundamental numbers that describe the properties, evolution, and fate of our universe within the framework of the theory of relativity and cosmology. This lesson will guide you through the most important parameters, their physical meanings, real-world examples, and their practical applications in understanding the cosmos.

1. What Are Cosmological Parameters?

Cosmological parameters quantify aspects such as:

  • The expansion rate of the universe
  • The geometry and curvature of space
  • The composition of the universe (matter, radiation, dark energy)
  • The age and ultimate fate of the cosmos

These parameters appear in the famous Friedmann equations derived from General Relativity, which govern the dynamics of the expanding universe.

Fun Fact: Cosmological parameters are continuously refined as observational technology improves, especially thanks to satellites like the Planck observatory and telescopes such as Hubble.

2. The Key Cosmological Parameters

2.1 The Hubble Constant \(H_0\)

The Hubble constant \( H_0 \) measures the current rate of expansion of the universe:

$$ H_0 = \left(\frac{\dot{a}}{a}\right)_{t=t_0} $$

where \( a(t) \) is the scale factor and \( t_0 \) is the present time. It tells us how fast galaxies are moving away from each other per unit distance.

Real-World Example: If \( H_0 = 70 \, \mathrm{km/s/Mpc} \), then for every megaparsec (~3.26 million light-years), galaxies appear to recede at 70 km/s due to cosmic expansion.

2.2 Density Parameters \( \Omega \)

Density parameters represent how different energy components contribute to the total energy density of the universe, compared to a critical density \( \rho_c \).

$$ \Omega_i = \frac{\rho_i}{\rho_c} $$

Here, \( \rho_i \) is the density of component \( i \) (like matter, radiation, dark energy), and \( \rho_c = \frac{3 H_0^2}{8 \pi G} \) is the critical density required for a flat universe.

  • \( \Omega_m \): Matter density (normal + dark matter)
  • \( \Omega_r \): Radiation density (light, photons)
  • \( \Omega_\Lambda \): Dark energy density (cosmological constant)
  • \( \Omega_k \): Curvature density parameter; measures spatial curvature

The sum of all density parameters determines the overall geometry of the universe:

$$ \Omega_m + \Omega_r + \Omega_\Lambda + \Omega_k = 1 $$

2.3 The Deceleration Parameter \( q_0 \)

The deceleration parameter tells us whether the expansion of the universe is speeding up or slowing down:

$$ q_0 = -\frac{\ddot{a} a}{\dot{a}^2} $$

A positive \( q_0 \) means the expansion is slowing down (decelerating), while a negative \( q_0 \) means acceleration.

3. Practical Applications of Cosmological Parameters

Scientists use these parameters to:

  • Predict the universe's age
  • Understand the fate of the universe — will it expand forever or collapse?
  • Model the Cosmic Microwave Background radiation
  • Calculate distances to faraway galaxies

Example: Calculating the Critical Density

Using the Hubble constant, the critical density is:

$$ \rho_c = \frac{3 H_0^2}{8 \pi G} $$ 
Given: 
  \( H_0 = 70 \, \mathrm{km/s/Mpc} = 2.27 \times 10^{-18} \, \mathrm{s}^{-1} \),
  \( G = 6.674 \times 10^{-11} \, \mathrm{m}^3\mathrm{kg}^{-1}\mathrm{s}^{-2} \).

Calculation:
  \( \rho_c = \frac{3 (2.27 \times 10^{-18})^2}{8 \pi (6.674 \times 10^{-11})} \approx 8.5 \times 10^{-27} \, \mathrm{kg/m}^3 \).

This is an extremely low density — about 5 hydrogen atoms per cubic meter!
Fun Fact: Despite the universe being mostly empty, cosmological parameters help us understand its large-scale structure and behavior, revealing that dark energy makes up about 68% of the universe's total energy.
