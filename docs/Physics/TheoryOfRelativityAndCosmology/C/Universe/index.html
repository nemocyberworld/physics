1. Static Universe

In the early 20th century, before Edwin Hubble's discoveries, many scientists believed the universe was static ‚Äî meaning unchanging in size, neither expanding nor contracting.

Edwin Hubble‚Äôs observation in 1929 showed galaxies moving away from us, indicating the universe was expanding ‚Äî not static. This revolutionized cosmology.

3. Open and Closed Universe

The ultimate fate of the universe depends on its shape and density:

  • Closed Universe: Curved like a sphere. Gravity will eventually stop expansion, causing a ‚ÄúBig Crunch.‚Äù
  • Open Universe: Curved like a saddle (hyperbolic). The universe will expand forever, getting colder and emptier.
  • Flat Universe: Geometry is flat; expansion slows but never stops.
$$ \Omega = \frac{\rho}{\rho_c} $$

Where:

  • \( \Omega \) is the density parameter.

  • \( \rho_c \) is the critical density needed for a flat universe.

Example: Picture the Earth as a closed universe ‚Äî if you travel far enough in one direction, you come back to where you started. An open universe is like an infinite saddle-shaped surface where paths diverge forever.

Why does shape matter?

The universe's shape influences:

  • How light travels across vast distances.
  • The overall fate of cosmic expansion.
  • Structure formation like galaxies and clusters.
