Cosmology: Static Black Holes (Schwarzschild & Reissner-Nordström)

This lesson covers two fundamental types of static black holes in General Relativity: the Schwarzschild black hole and the Reissner-Nordström black hole. Both represent idealized solutions to Einstein's field equations, helping us understand gravity, spacetime curvature, and extreme astrophysical objects.

Schwarzschild Black Hole

The Schwarzschild black hole is the simplest solution describing a non-rotating, uncharged black hole — meaning it has mass but no electric charge or spin. It was discovered by Karl Schwarzschild in 1916, shortly after Einstein published General Relativity.

It describes a spherical black hole characterized only by its mass M. The event horizon — the boundary beyond which nothing can escape — lies at the Schwarzschild radius:

$$ R_s = \frac{2GM}{c^2} $$
Where: \(G\) is the gravitational constant, \(M\) the mass of the black hole, and \(c\) the speed of light.

For example, a black hole with the mass of our Sun would have a Schwarzschild radius of about 3 kilometers!

Given the Sun’s mass \( M_\odot = 1.989 \times 10^{30} \, \text{kg} \), the Schwarzschild radius is:

$$ R_s = \frac{2 \times 6.674 \times 10^{-11} \times 1.989 \times 10^{30}}{(3 \times 10^8)^2} \approx 2.95 \text{ km} $$

This tiny radius compared to the Sun’s actual size (~700,000 km) shows how dense black holes are!

Key Properties:

  • Non-rotating and uncharged
  • Spherically symmetric spacetime geometry
  • Event horizon at Schwarzschild radius
  • Singularity at the center where curvature becomes infinite

Physical Interpretation & Applications

Schwarzschild black holes provide a foundation for understanding gravitational time dilation, light bending, and black hole thermodynamics. They model static black holes formed from collapsed stars without spin or charge.

Real-World Example: Stellar-mass black holes detected via X-ray binaries often approximate Schwarzschild solutions if spin and charge are low.

Fun Fact: Schwarzschild’s original paper was written while he was serving on the Russian front during World War I!

Reissner-Nordström Black Hole

The Reissner-Nordström black hole generalizes Schwarzschild’s solution by including electric charge. It describes a static, spherically symmetric black hole with mass M and charge Q.

Its spacetime metric includes effects of electromagnetic fields, leading to more complex horizons. The radii of the horizons (outer and inner) are given by:

$$ r_\pm = \frac{GM}{c^2} \pm \sqrt{\left(\frac{GM}{c^2}\right)^2 - \frac{GQ^2}{4\pi \epsilon_0 c^4}} $$
Where: \(r_+\) is the event horizon radius, \(r_-\) the Cauchy horizon, \(Q\) is the electric charge, and \(\epsilon_0\) the permittivity of free space.

If the charge is zero, this reduces to the Schwarzschild radius. If the charge is large enough, the black hole becomes an extremal black hole with interesting physical properties.

For a black hole with mass \(M\) and charge \(Q\), depending on the balance of these values, it can have two horizons, one horizon, or none (a naked singularity).

This has profound implications in cosmic censorship and the stability of spacetime.

Key Properties:

  • Static, spherically symmetric, but electrically charged
  • Two horizons: event horizon and Cauchy (inner) horizon
  • Charge affects gravitational field and horizon structure
  • Potential for extremal black holes where horizons merge

Physical Interpretation & Applications

Reissner-Nordström black holes are theoretical but provide insight into how charge affects black hole geometry and thermodynamics. They are crucial in advanced gravitational theories and quantum gravity research.

Fun Fact: Although astrophysical black holes likely have negligible net charge, charged black holes are important in string theory and holography!

Summary

Static black holes are idealized but foundational solutions in General Relativity.
Schwarzschild black holes describe neutral, non-rotating black holes with a single event horizon.
Reissner-Nordström black holes add electric charge, introducing richer horizon structures.

Both solutions deepen our understanding of gravity, spacetime, and the extreme conditions found near black holes.
