Tensor Calculus: Meaning of Parallel Transport and Covariant Derivatives

Introduction

In curved spaces, like the fabric of space-time described by Einstein’s theory of relativity, the usual way we differentiate vectors and move them around doesn't work. We need tools that take into account the curvature of space. Two such tools are parallel transport and covariant derivatives.

Fun Fact: On a sphere, if you move a vector around a triangle on the surface, it comes back rotated — even though it never "spun" on its own!

What is Parallel Transport?

Imagine walking around the Earth with an arrow pointing forward. If you go along the equator, then up a line of longitude, and finally return along another latitude, the arrow ends up pointing in a different direction — even though it was always pointing “forward.” This is called parallel transport.

$$ \text{Parallel transport preserves the vector’s length and direction locally, but not globally in curved space.} $$

Covariant Derivative

A covariant derivative allows us to differentiate vectors and tensors in curved space by accounting for the change in the coordinate system itself. It corrects for the curvature and ensures the result is a proper tensor.

$$ \nabla_j V^i = \partial_j V^i + \Gamma^i_{jk} V^k $$

Here:

  • \( \nabla_j V^i \) is the covariant derivative of vector \( V^i \)
  • \( \partial_j V^i \) is the partial derivative
  • \( \Gamma^i_{jk} \) are Christoffel symbols, which account for curvature

Real World Applications

  • General Relativity: Describes how mass curves spacetime using covariant derivatives.
  • Orbital mechanics: Predicts how planets move in curved space.
  • Engineering: Used in stress analysis on curved surfaces like airplane wings or domes.
  • Computer graphics: Simulating cloth or terrain deformation.
