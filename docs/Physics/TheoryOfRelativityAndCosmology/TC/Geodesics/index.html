Tensor Calculus: Geodesics and Autoparallel Curves

Introduction

In flat space, the shortest distance between two points is a straight line. But what about on a sphere or in curved space-time? The equivalent of a "straight line" is called a geodesic. These curves follow the shape of the space and are central to general relativity. Closely related are autoparallel curves, paths that preserve direction under parallel transport.

Fun Fact: The path followed by light near a massive object, like a black hole, is a geodesic bent by space-time curvature!

What is a Geodesic?

A geodesic is the curve that represents the path of least action (or extremal path) in curved space. In general relativity, free-falling objects move along geodesics in space-time.

$$ \frac{d^2 x^\mu}{d\tau^2} + \Gamma^\mu_{\nu \lambda} \frac{d x^\nu}{d\tau} \frac{d x^\lambda}{d\tau} = 0 $$

This is called the geodesic equation, where:

  • \( x^\mu \) is the coordinate of the particle
  • \( \tau \) is the proper time
  • \( \Gamma^\mu_{\nu \lambda} \) are the Christoffel symbols representing curvature

Autoparallel Curves

An autoparallel curve is one in which the tangent vector is parallel transported along itself. For geodesics defined by an affine parameter (like proper time), they are equivalent.

$$ \nabla_{\vec{v}} \vec{v} = 0 $$

In this form, it means that the vector \( \vec{v} \) (the velocity) doesn’t "twist" as it moves — it stays tangent to the curve.

Real World Examples

  • Airline routes: Aircraft fly along geodesics on the Earth’s surface (great circles).
  • GPS systems: Calculate time and position corrections using space-time geodesics.
  • General Relativity: Planets and light follow geodesics in curved space-time.
  • Computer vision: Geodesics are used in image segmentation and 3D object recognition.
