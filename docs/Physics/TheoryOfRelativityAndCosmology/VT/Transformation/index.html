Vectors and Tensors: Covariant, Contravariant, and Mixed Tensors

Chapter: Transformation Properties of Rank-2 Tensors

This lesson will explore the important concepts of covariant, contravariant, and mixed tensors of rank-2, especially their transformation properties under coordinate changes. These concepts are fundamental in Theory of Relativity and Cosmology.

1. Introduction to Vectors and Tensors

Before diving into tensors, let's recall vectors. In physics, a vector is an object with both magnitude and direction that transforms in a specific way under coordinate changes. Tensors are generalizations of vectors — they are multi-dimensional arrays that transform under coordinate changes according to well-defined rules.

1.1 Covariant and Contravariant Vectors

There are two types of vectors based on their transformation behavior:

  • Contravariant vectors (components with superscripts, e.g., \( V^i \)) transform like basis vectors.
  • Covariant vectors (components with subscripts, e.g., \( V_i \)) transform like dual basis vectors (or gradients).

Intuitively, contravariant vectors can be thought of as arrows pointing in space, while covariant vectors act like measuring sticks or gradients.

$$ V'^i = \frac{\partial x'^i}{\partial x^j} V^j $$

This is the transformation rule for a contravariant vector.

$$ V'_i = \frac{\partial x^j}{\partial x'^i} V_j $$

This is the transformation rule for a covariant vector.

1.2 Fun Fact

Did you know? The terms covariant and contravariant were introduced in the early 20th century to precisely describe how different mathematical objects change under coordinate transformations — a crucial step for Einstein's formulation of General Relativity!

2. Rank-2 Tensors

A rank-2 tensor is an object with two indices, such as \( T^{ij} \), \( T_{ij} \), or mixed \( T^i_j \). These indices can be covariant (subscript) or contravariant (superscript). Rank-2 tensors can represent physical quantities like:

  • The metric tensor \( g_{ij} \), which defines distances and angles in space-time.
  • The stress-energy tensor \( T^{\mu\nu} \), which represents the density and flux of energy and momentum.

2.1 Types of Rank-2 Tensors

  • Covariant tensors \( T_{ij} \): Both indices are subscripts.
  • Contravariant tensors \( T^{ij} \): Both indices are superscripts.
  • Mixed tensors \( T^i_j \): One superscript and one subscript.

2.2 Transformation Properties

Let’s see how these tensors transform under a change of coordinates \( x^i \to x'^i \):

Covariant tensor:

$$ T'_{ij} = \frac{\partial x^k}{\partial x'^i} \frac{\partial x^l}{\partial x'^j} T_{kl} $$

Contravariant tensor:

$$ T'^{ij} = \frac{\partial x'^i}{\partial x^k} \frac{\partial x'^j}{\partial x^l} T^{kl} $$

Mixed tensor:

$$ T'^i_j = \frac{\partial x'^i}{\partial x^k} \frac{\partial x^l}{\partial x'^j} T^k_l $$

3. Real-World Example: Stress-Energy Tensor in Relativity

The stress-energy tensor \( T^{\mu\nu} \) in General Relativity encodes the density and flow of energy and momentum in space-time. It is a contravariant rank-2 tensor.

Physically, this tensor tells us how matter and energy influence the curvature of space-time, leading to gravity.

3.1 Practical Application

In astrophysics, the stress-energy tensor helps predict how black holes and neutron stars warp the fabric of space-time, influencing light paths and gravitational waves.

5. Summary

- Covariant and contravariant indices indicate how tensors transform under coordinate changes.

- Rank-2 tensors come in three flavors: covariant (\( T_{ij} \)), contravariant (\( T^{ij} \)), and mixed (\( T^i_j \)).

- Understanding transformation properties is crucial for correctly applying tensors in physics, especially in relativity.
