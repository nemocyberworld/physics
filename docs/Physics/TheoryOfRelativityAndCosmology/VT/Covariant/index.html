Covariant and Contravariant Vectors

Vectors and Tensors in Theory of Relativity and Cosmology

In the realms of Theory of Relativity and Cosmology, vectors and tensors play crucial roles. But unlike the simple vectors you might be familiar with, such as displacement or velocity vectors, here we deal with vectors that transform in more complex ways under coordinate changes â€” namely, covariant and contravariant vectors.

1. What Are Covariant and Contravariant Vectors?

Simply put, covariant and contravariant vectors describe how components of vectors change when you switch coordinate systems, especially in curved spaces like spacetime in relativity.

  • Contravariant Vectors: Think of these as "normal" vectors that transform directly with the change of coordinates.
  • Covariant Vectors (or Covectors): These transform in the opposite way and are often related to gradients and dual spaces.

If you have a coordinate transformation represented by x^{i} \to x^{i'}, then the components of contravariant vectors transform as:

$$ V^{i'} = \frac{\partial x^{i'}}{\partial x^{j}} V^{j} $$

While the components of covariant vectors transform as:

$$ W_{i'} = \frac{\partial x^{j}}{\partial x^{i'}} W_{j} $$

Notice the inverse nature of the partial derivatives in these formulas, which defines their "contra-" vs "co-" variance.

2. Why Does This Matter in Relativity?

Einsteinâ€™s General Theory of Relativity describes gravity as curvature of spacetime. To describe physics in this curved spacetime, we must use mathematical objects (vectors and tensors) that transform correctly under changes in coordinates â€” since the coordinates themselves can be chosen arbitrarily.

Contravariant vectors can represent quantities like velocities or displacements, while covariant vectors can represent gradients or forces.

Example: Imagine you're tracking a spaceship traveling near a black hole. Its velocity vector changes depending on your coordinate system. The contravariant components describe how fast and in which direction it moves. The covariant components, on the other hand, relate to gravitational potential gradients felt by the ship.

4. Mathematical Framework: Dual Spaces

In linear algebra, every vector space has a dual space, consisting of linear functions mapping vectors to scalars. Covariant vectors live in this dual space, while contravariant vectors live in the original vector space.

For example, a contravariant vector might be represented by components \(V^{i}\), while a covariant vector is represented by components \(W_{i}\), where the lowering and raising of indices is done using a metric tensor \(g_{ij}\):

$$ W_{i} = g_{ij} V^{j} $$

The metric tensor acts like a bridge that converts contravariant vectors into covariant vectors and vice versa.
