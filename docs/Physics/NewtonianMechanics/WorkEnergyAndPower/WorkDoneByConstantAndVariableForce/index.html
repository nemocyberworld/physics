Physics Topic: Work Done by Constant and Variable Forces

1. Introduction to Work

In physics, work is done when a force is applied to an object and the object moves in the direction of the applied force. It is a scalar quantity and has the unit of joules (J) in the SI system.

$$ \text{Work} = \text{Force} \times \text{Displacement} \times \cos(\theta) $$

Where:

  • Force is in newtons (N)
  • Displacement is in meters (m)
  • \(\theta\) is the angle between force and displacement

2. Work Done by a Constant Force

When a constant force is applied in a specific direction, and the object moves a certain distance, the work done is straightforward to calculate.

$$ W = F \cdot d \cdot \cos(\theta) $$

Real-world Example

If you push a shopping cart with a force of 50 N over a distance of 10 meters in the same direction, the work done is:

$$ W = 50 \times 10 \times \cos(0^\circ) = 500 \text{ J} $$

Practical Applications

  • Moving furniture across a room
  • Pulling a sled on snow
  • Using a wrench to turn a bolt

3. Work Done by a Variable Force

When the force changes over the distance, we cannot use a simple multiplication. Instead, we integrate the force over the path of motion.

$$ W = \int_{x_i}^{x_f} F(x) \, dx $$

Real-world Example

Imagine stretching a spring. The force required increases linearly with distance:

$$ F(x) = kx $$

Then the work done in stretching it from 0 to x is:

$$ W = \int_0^x kx \, dx = \frac{1}{2} k x^2 $$

Applications of Variable Force Work

  • Stretching or compressing springs (Hooke's Law)
  • Rocket propulsion (force varies as fuel burns)
  • Gravitational potential energy changes with altitude in non-uniform fields

4. Important Notes and Insights

  • If \( \theta = 90^\circ \), then \( \cos(\theta) = 0 \), and no work is done (e.g., carrying a bag horizontally while walking)
  • Work can be positive (force and displacement in same direction), negative (opposite directions), or zero

5. Fun Fact

Did you know that holding a heavy bag without moving it might feel tiring, but in physics, you’re doing no work? Since there’s no displacement, no work is technically done!

6. Graphical Interpretation

When force varies, the area under the Force vs. Displacement graph gives the total work done. For constant force, it’s a rectangle; for variable, it could be a triangle or other shapes, integrated accordingly.
