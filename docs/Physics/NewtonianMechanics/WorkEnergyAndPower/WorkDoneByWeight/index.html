Physics Topic: Work Done by Weight

1. Introduction

In physics, the concept of work done by weight is fundamental when dealing with gravity-related problems. This topic falls under the broader chapter of Work, Energy, and Power. Here, we explore how gravity does work when objects are moved vertically or fall freely.

2. What is Weight?

Weight is the force exerted by gravity on a body. It is given by:

$$ W = mg $$

where:

  • W is the weight of the object (in newtons, N)
  • m is the mass (in kilograms, kg)
  • g is the acceleration due to gravity (approximately \( 9.8\,m/s^2 \) on Earth)

3. Definition of Work

Work is said to be done when a force causes displacement in the direction of the force. Mathematically, it is given by:

$$ W = F \cdot d \cdot \cos(\theta) $$

where:

  • W is the work done (in joules, J)
  • F is the force applied
  • d is the displacement
  • \theta is the angle between force and displacement

4. Work Done by Weight

When an object moves vertically under the influence of gravity, the weight does work. The work done by weight is:

$$ W = mg \cdot h $$

where h is the height (positive if the object moves downward, negative if upward).

5. Real-World Examples

  • Falling Apple: When an apple falls from a tree, gravity does work by increasing the apple’s kinetic energy.
  • Lifting a Box: When you lift a box upward, you work against the weight, and the weight does negative work.
  • Roller Coasters: As the car descends, gravity does work, increasing the speed of the car.
  • Skydiving: Gravity does work on the skydiver, converting potential energy to kinetic energy.

6. Practical Applications

  • Construction: Engineers calculate work done by weight when designing cranes and lifts.
  • Hydropower: Gravity does work on falling water to rotate turbines and generate electricity.
  • Transportation: In vehicles, work done by weight affects downhill acceleration and energy conservation.
  • Elevators: The motor must do work equal to the work done by weight to raise passengers.

7. Energy Perspective

When weight does work, potential energy is converted to kinetic energy. If an object of mass m falls a height h:

$$ \text{Potential Energy Lost} = \text{Kinetic Energy Gained} = mgh $$

This is an application of the Work-Energy Theorem.

8. Fun Fact!

The Eiffel Tower is 330 meters tall. If you dropped a bowling ball (mass 6 kg) from the top, the work done by gravity would be:

$$ W = 6 \cdot 9.8 \cdot 330 = 19,404\,J $$

That’s enough energy to light a 60W bulb for over 5 minutes!

9. Key Points to Remember

  • Weight is a force: \( W = mg \)
  • Work by weight: \( W = mgh \)
  • Work is positive when moving downward, negative when moving upward
  • Gravity does work by converting potential energy to kinetic energy
  • Work done by weight is foundational in machines, transport, and natural processes

10. Final Example Problem

Problem: A 10 kg suitcase is lifted to a height of 2 meters. Calculate the work done by weight.

Solution:

$$ W = mg \cdot h = 10 \cdot 9.8 \cdot (-2) = -196\,J $$

The negative sign shows that work done by weight is against the direction of motion.
