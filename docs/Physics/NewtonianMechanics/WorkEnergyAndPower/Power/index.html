Power

Power is a fundamental concept in physics that describes how quickly work is done or energy is transferred. It's crucial in understanding machines, engines, and even our own bodies!

Definition of Power

Power is defined as the rate at which work is done or energy is transferred over time.

$$ P = \frac{W}{t} $$

Where:

  • P = Power (in Watts)
  • W = Work done or Energy transferred (in Joules)
  • t = Time taken (in seconds)

Unit of Power

The SI unit of power is the Watt (W).

1 Watt = 1 Joule/second

Other common units:

  • Kilowatt (kW) = 1000 W
  • Horsepower (hp) ≈ 746 W

Real-World Examples

1. Light Bulb

A 60W light bulb uses 60 joules of energy per second.

2. Human Body

An average person uses about 100 watts of power when resting. That’s like powering a light bulb just by existing!

3. Electric Kettle

A typical kettle uses about 2000W (2kW) of power to boil water quickly.

4. Cars

Car engines are often rated in horsepower. A car with 150 hp can produce about 112 kW of power.

Power in Terms of Force and Velocity

If an object is moving with a velocity v while a constant force F is applied, power can also be calculated as:

$$ P = F \cdot v $$

Practical Applications

  • Engineering: Designing engines, turbines, and electric motors.
  • Home Appliances: Calculating electricity usage and energy bills.
  • Sports: Evaluating athlete performance (e.g., cyclists often measure power output).
  • Health: Calorimetry uses power output to measure metabolic rates.

Fun Fact

The term "horsepower" was invented by James Watt to market his steam engines. He wanted to show how many horses his engine could replace!

Example Problems

Example 1: Power of a Weightlifter

A weightlifter lifts a 200 N weight to a height of 2 m in 2 seconds. What is the power output?

$$ Work done = Force × Distance = 200 × 2 = 400 J
Power = \frac{400}{2} = 200 \text{ W} $$

Example 2: Energy Consumption of an Appliance

If a heater has a power rating of 1500 W and it runs for 3 hours, how much energy does it consume?

$$ Energy = Power × Time = 1500 × 3 × 3600 = 16,200,000 J = 4.5 kWh $$

Example 3: Car Engine Power

A car engine applies a force of 3000 N and moves at a velocity of 20 m/s. What is the power output?

$$ P = F × v = 3000 × 20 = 60,000 W = 60 kW $$
