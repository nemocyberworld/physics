1. Vector Algebra

Vectors are mathematical objects that have both magnitude and direction. They're used extensively in physics and engineering to describe forces, velocities, and other directional quantities.

Example 1: Vector Addition

Given vectors \( \mathbf{A} = 3\hat{i} + 2\hat{j} \) and \( \mathbf{B} = 4\hat{i} - \hat{j} \), find \( \mathbf{A} + \mathbf{B} \).

\( \mathbf{A} + \mathbf{B} = (3 + 4)\hat{i} + (2 - 1)\hat{j} = 7\hat{i} + \hat{j} \)

Real-life application: Adding velocity vectors in flight navigation.

4. Scalar (Dot) Product

The scalar product of two vectors is a number (scalar) obtained by multiplying the magnitudes of the two vectors and the cosine of the angle between them.

$$ \mathbf{A} \cdot \mathbf{B} = |\mathbf{A}||\mathbf{B}| \cos\theta $$

Real-world example: The work done by a force is the dot product of the force vector and displacement vector.

5. Vector (Cross) Product

The vector product (or cross product) results in a vector perpendicular to both original vectors.

$$ \mathbf{A} \times \mathbf{B} = |\mathbf{A}||\mathbf{B}|\sin\theta\ \hat{n} $$

Application: Used in torque calculation: \( \tau = \mathbf{r} \times \mathbf{F} \)

Example 16: Compute \( \mathbf{A} \times \mathbf{B} \)

Let \( \mathbf{A} = \hat{i} + 2\hat{j} + 3\hat{k} \), \( \mathbf{B} = 4\hat{i} + 5\hat{j} + 6\hat{k} \)

$$ \mathbf{A} \times \mathbf{B} = \left|\begin{array}{ccc} \hat{i} & \hat{j} & \hat{k} \\ 1 & 2 & 3 \\ 4 & 5 & 6 \end{array}\right| $$

Result: \( \mathbf{A} \times \mathbf{B} = -3\hat{i} + 6\hat{j} - 3\hat{k} \)

Example 17: Magnitude of Cross Product

\( \mathbf{A} = 2\hat{i} + \hat{j}, \mathbf{B} = 3\hat{i} + 4\hat{j} \)

$$ |\mathbf{A} \times \mathbf{B}| = |(2)(4) - (1)(3)| = 5 $$

Example 18: Parallel Vectors

If \( \mathbf{A} \parallel \mathbf{B} \), then \( \theta = 0^{\circ} \) or \( 180^{\circ} \), and \( \sin\theta = 0 \), hence \( \mathbf{A} \times \mathbf{B} = 0 \).

Example 20: Invariance

The scalar triple product is invariant under cyclic permutations:

$$ \mathbf{A} \cdot (\mathbf{B} \times \mathbf{C}) = \mathbf{B} \cdot (\mathbf{C} \times \mathbf{A}) = \mathbf{C} \cdot (\mathbf{A} \times \mathbf{B}) $$

7. Vector Triple Product

The vector triple product involves three vectors and can be simplified using an identity.

$$ \mathbf{A} \times (\mathbf{B} \times \mathbf{C}) = (\mathbf{A} \cdot \mathbf{C})\mathbf{B} - (\mathbf{A} \cdot \mathbf{B})\mathbf{C} $$

Example 21:

\( \mathbf{A} = \hat{i} + \hat{j}, \mathbf{B} = 2\hat{i} - \hat{j}, \mathbf{C} = 3\hat{i} + 4\hat{k} \)

Example 22: Proof of Identity

To prove the identity

\( \mathbf{A} \times (\mathbf{B} \times \mathbf{C}) = (\mathbf{A} \cdot \mathbf{C})\mathbf{B} - (\mathbf{A} \cdot \mathbf{B})\mathbf{C} \)
consider expanding
\( \mathbf{B} \times \mathbf{C} \)
into its components, then take the cross product with \( \mathbf{A} \). By regrouping and simplifying terms, you’ll arrive at the right-hand side of the identity. This is extremely useful in simplifying expressions in physics and engineering involving torque, angular momentum, and electromagnetic field equations.

🌐 Volume in Spherical Coordinates

The volume element in spherical coordinates is:

$$ dV = r^2 \sin(\phi)\,dr\,d\theta\,d\phi $$

To compute the volume of a sphere of radius \(R\):

$$ V = \int_0^{2\pi} \int_0^{\pi} \int_0^R r^2 \sin(\phi)\,dr\,d\phi\,d\theta $$
Real-World Example: Calculating the volume of a planet, modeled as a sphere, helps astronomers estimate its mass when the average density is known.

📌 Convert Spherical to Cartesian Coordinates

Given: \((r=4, \theta=\frac{\pi}{3}, \phi=\frac{\pi}{6})\)

$$ x = r \sin(\phi) \cos(\theta),\quad y = r \sin(\phi) \sin(\theta),\quad z = r \cos(\phi) $$

🧭 Cartesian to Spherical Coordinates

Given: \((x, y, z) = (2, 2, 2)\)

$$ r = \sqrt{x^2 + y^2 + z^2}, \quad \theta = \tan^{-1}(\frac{y}{x}), \quad \phi = \cos^{-1}(\frac{z}{r}) $$

Example 83: Prove if cross product is zero, vectors are parallel or anti-parallel

The cross product of two vectors is zero if and only if the vectors are collinear (parallel or anti-parallel). Why?

Recall: The magnitude of the cross product is given by

$$ |\mathbf{A} \times \mathbf{B}| = |\mathbf{A}||\mathbf{B}|\sin\theta $$

If the cross product is zero, then either:

  • At least one vector is zero (trivial case)
  • Or \(\sin \theta = 0\) which means \(\theta = 0^\circ\) or \(180^\circ\)

So, the vectors lie along the same or exactly opposite direction — they are parallel or anti-parallel.

Example 84: Find angle between two vectors using scalar product

Vectors:

  • \(\mathbf{A} = 3\hat{i} + 4\hat{j}\)
  • \(\mathbf{B} = -\hat{i} + 2\hat{j}\)

Angle \(\theta\) between vectors can be found by:

$$ \cos \theta = \frac{\mathbf{A} \cdot \mathbf{B}}{|\mathbf{A}||\mathbf{B}|} $$

Calculate step by step:

  • \(\mathbf{A} \cdot \mathbf{B} = 3 \times (-1) + 4 \times 2 = -3 + 8 = 5\)
  • \(|\mathbf{A}| = \sqrt{3^2 + 4^2} = 5\)
  • \(|\mathbf{B}| = \sqrt{(-1)^2 + 2^2} = \sqrt{5}\)

Then:

$$ \cos \theta = \frac{5}{5 \times \sqrt{5}} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{5}} $$

So, \(\theta = \cos^{-1}\left(\frac{1}{\sqrt{5}}\right) \approx 63.43^\circ\).

32. Vector Identities

Identity 88: Vector identity involving cross products

Prove that:

$$(\mathbf{A} \times \mathbf{B}) \cdot (\mathbf{A} \times \mathbf{B}) = |\mathbf{A}|^2 |\mathbf{B}|^2 - (\mathbf{A} \cdot \mathbf{B})^2$$

This identity shows that the square of the magnitude of the cross product equals the product of the squared magnitudes minus the square of the dot product.

This is related to the geometric fact that:

$$|\mathbf{A} \times \mathbf{B}| = |\mathbf{A}||\mathbf{B}|\sin \theta$$

and

$$\mathbf{A} \cdot \mathbf{B} = |\mathbf{A}||\mathbf{B}|\cos \theta$$

Since \(\sin^2 \theta + \cos^2 \theta = 1\), the identity follows naturally.

Example 95: Scalar triple product and volume of parallelepiped

Vectors:

  • \(\mathbf{A} = \hat{i} + 2\hat{j} + 3\hat{k}\)
  • \(\mathbf{B} = 4\hat{i} + 5\hat{j} + 6\hat{k}\)
  • \(\mathbf{C} = 7\hat{i} + 8\hat{j} + 9\hat{k}\)

The scalar triple product is:

$$ \mathbf{A} \cdot (\mathbf{B} \times \mathbf{C}) $$

Which equals the volume of the parallelepiped formed by the three vectors.

35. Applications of Vector Algebra

Example 99: Using vector products in physics (Torque)

Torque \(\boldsymbol{\tau}\) is given by:

$$ \boldsymbol{\tau} = \mathbf{r} \times \mathbf{F} $$

where \(\mathbf{r}\) is the position vector from the pivot point, and \(\mathbf{F}\) is the applied force vector.
