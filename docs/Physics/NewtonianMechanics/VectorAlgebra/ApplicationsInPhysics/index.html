Physics Topic: Applications of Vectors in Physics

1. Position, Displacement, Velocity, and Acceleration Vectors

In physics, motion is described using vectors. A vector is a quantity that has both magnitude and direction.

Position Vector

The position vector \( \vec{r} \) describes the location of an object relative to an origin.

$$ \vec{r} = x \hat{i} + y \hat{j} + z \hat{k} $$

Displacement Vector

Displacement is the change in position:

$$ \Delta\vec{r} = \vec{r}_f - \vec{r}_i $$

Velocity Vector

Velocity is the rate of change of position:

$$ \vec{v} = \frac{d\vec{r}}{dt} $$

Acceleration Vector

Acceleration is the rate of change of velocity:

$$ \vec{a} = \frac{d\vec{v}}{dt} $$

Real World Example

A car moves from point A to point B, 300 meters east and then 400 meters north. The displacement vector is:

\( \vec{d} = 300\hat{i} + 400\hat{j} \) meters

The magnitude is:

$$ |\vec{d}| = \sqrt{300^2 + 400^2} = 500 \text{ meters} $$

This is the direct distance from A to B, like the hypotenuse of a right triangle.

2. Force, Momentum, and Angular Momentum

Force Vector

Newton's Second Law gives the force:

$$ \vec{F} = m \vec{a} $$

Momentum

Momentum is the product of mass and velocity:

$$ \vec{p} = m \vec{v} $$

Angular Momentum

Angular momentum is:

$$ \vec{L} = \vec{r} \times \vec{p} $$

Real World Example

Imagine a soccer player kicks a 0.5 kg ball at 10 m/s. The momentum is:

\( \vec{p} = 0.5 \times 10 = 5 \ \text{kg·m/s} \)

If the ball is kicked from 2 meters from a point of rotation, angular momentum is:

$$ L = r p \sin(\theta) = 2 \times 5 \times \sin(90^\circ) = 10 \ \text{kg·m}^2/\text{s} $$

3. Electric and Magnetic Field Vectors

Electric Field

The electric field created by a point charge is:

$$ \vec{E} = k_e \frac{q}{r^2} \hat{r} $$

Magnetic Field

Magnetic field around a straight wire carrying current:

$$ \vec{B} = \frac{\mu_0 I}{2\pi r} $$

Real World Example

Suppose a charged balloon (1 µC) is 0.2 meters away from a point in space. The electric field is:

$$ E = 9 \times 10^9 \times \frac{1 \times 10^{-6}}{0.2^2} = 225000 \ \text{N/C} $$

This field would influence any nearby charged objects.

4. Torque and Moment of Force

Torque

Torque is the rotational equivalent of force:

$$ \vec{\tau} = \vec{r} \times \vec{F} $$

Moment of Force

This is another term for torque, often used in engineering:

$$ M = F \cdot d \cdot \sin(\theta) $$

Real World Example

You use a wrench to tighten a bolt. If you apply a 20 N force at the end of a 0.3 m wrench, perpendicular to it:

$$ \tau = 0.3 \times 20 = 6 \ \text{N·m} $$

This torque is what allows you to turn the bolt.
