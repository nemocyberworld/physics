Relation Between Angular Momentum and Torque

Chapter: Collisions of Bodies

Introduction

In the study of rotational motion, understanding the relationship between angular momentum and torque is fundamental. This relationship plays a crucial role in analyzing rotational systems, especially during and after collisions. In this lesson, we will explore the in-depth connection between angular momentum and torque, illustrate it with equations, examples, and real-world applications, and show its importance in physical systems.

1. What is Angular Momentum?

Angular momentum (denoted by \(\vec{L}\)) is the rotational analog of linear momentum. It is a vector quantity and is defined for a rotating body as:

$$ \vec{L} = \vec{r} \times \vec{p} = \vec{r} \times m\vec{v} $$

Where:

  • \(\vec{r}\) is the position vector from the axis of rotation to the object
  • \(m\) is the mass of the object
  • \(\vec{v}\) is the linear velocity
  • \(\times\) denotes the cross product

2. What is Torque?

Torque (denoted by \(\vec{\tau}\)) is the measure of the rotational force acting on a body. It changes the rotational state (angular velocity or angular momentum) of an object. Torque is given by:

$$ \vec{\tau} = \vec{r} \times \vec{F} $$

Where:

  • \(\vec{F}\) is the force applied
  • \(\vec{r}\) is the position vector from the axis of rotation to the point of application of the force

3. The Fundamental Relation

The torque on an object is equal to the time rate of change of its angular momentum:

$$ \vec{\tau} = \frac{d\vec{L}}{dt} $$

This is the rotational analog of Newton's Second Law for linear motion (\(F = dp/dt\)). It tells us that:

  • If no external torque acts on a system, its angular momentum remains constant (conservation of angular momentum).
  • A non-zero torque leads to a change in angular momentum.

4. Application in Collisions

During collisions, particularly rotational ones, torque plays a significant role. Consider the case of a spinning disk struck off-center. The impact applies a torque, changing its angular momentum. In the absence of external torques, the total angular momentum before and after the collision is conserved.

5. Real-World Examples

A. Figure Skater

When a figure skater pulls in their arms, their moment of inertia decreases. Since angular momentum is conserved:

$$ L = I \omega = \text{constant} $$

So, a decrease in moment of inertia \(I\) leads to an increase in angular velocity \(\omega\).

B. Bicycles and Gyroscopes

The spinning wheels of a bicycle create angular momentum. When you try to tilt a moving bicycle, torque is generated, causing precession rather than tipping. This is the basis of gyroscopic stability.

C. Spacecraft Orientation

Satellites and spacecraft use conservation of angular momentum to orient themselves in space using reaction wheels. Applying torque in one direction rotates the spacecraft in the opposite direction.

6. Practical Applications

  • Mechanical Engineering: Torque and angular momentum are central in gear design and rotational engines.
  • Aerospace: Stabilizing satellites using reaction wheels and momentum gyros.
  • Sports: Gymnasts, divers, and ice skaters manipulate angular momentum for flips and spins.
  • Robotics: Torque control is crucial for articulated robotic arms.

8. Summary

  • Angular Momentum is a vector quantity representing rotational motion.
  • Torque is the cause of angular acceleration and changes in angular momentum.
  • Relation: \(\vec{\tau} = \frac{d\vec{L}}{dt}\)
  • In the absence of external torque, angular momentum is conserved.
  • Used in engineering, space science, sports, and more.
