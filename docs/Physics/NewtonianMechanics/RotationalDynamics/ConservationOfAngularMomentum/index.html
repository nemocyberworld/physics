Conservation of Angular Momentum

Angular momentum is a measure of the rotational motion of an object. The conservation of angular momentum is a fundamental principle in physics that states:

$$ \text{Total angular momentum before collision} = \text{Total angular momentum after collision} $$

This principle holds true in the absence of external torques acting on a system.

1. Understanding Angular Momentum

Angular momentum (L) of a rotating object is given by the equation:

$$ L = I \omega $$

Where:

  • L = Angular momentum
  • I = Moment of inertia
  • \( \omega \) = Angular velocity

Moment of Inertia

The moment of inertia depends on the mass of the object and how that mass is distributed about the axis of rotation.

2. Real-World Examples

Ice Skater

When a spinning ice skater pulls in their arms, they reduce their moment of inertia and spin faster. This is a classic example of conservation of angular momentum.

Planetary Motion

Planets orbiting the sun maintain constant angular momentum. As a planet moves closer to the sun (perihelion), it speeds up, and when it is farther (aphelion), it slows down.

Rotating Chair Experiment

Sit on a rotating chair with dumbbells in your hands stretched out. When you pull your hands in, the chair spins faster due to the conservation of angular momentum.

Neutron Stars

When massive stars collapse into neutron stars, their radius drastically reduces, increasing their spin rate due to conservation of angular momentum.

3. Angular Momentum in Collisions

During collisions, especially rotational collisions, angular momentum is conserved if no external torque acts on the system. This is especially important in:

  • Spinning discs sticking together
  • Rotating machinery parts during sudden stoppage or engagement
  • Astrophysical phenomena such as merging black holes or galaxies

Example Problem

Problem: A rotating disc of moment of inertia \( I_1 = 5 \ \text{kg} \cdot \text{m}^2 \) and angular velocity \( \omega_1 = 4 \ \text{rad/s} \) collides with another disc of moment of inertia \( I_2 = 3 \ \text{kg} \cdot \text{m}^2 \) and \( \omega_2 = 0 \). What is the final angular velocity after the discs stick together?

Solution:

$$ L_{\text{initial}} = I_1 \omega_1 + I_2 \omega_2 = 5 \times 4 + 3 \times 0 = 20 $$
$$ L_{\text{final}} = (I_1 + I_2) \omega_f $$
$$ 20 = (5 + 3) \omega_f $$ $$ \omega_f = \frac{20}{8} = 2.5 \ \text{rad/s} $$

4. Practical Applications

  • Gyroscopes in aircraft navigation systems
  • Spin stabilization in satellites
  • Wheel alignment in vehicles
  • Control of robotics arms in industry
  • Spinning tools in mechanical workshops

5. Fun Fact!

Did you know that a figure skater can increase their spin speed up to 4 times just by pulling in their arms? Olympic skaters often use this trick to impress judges during performances!

6. Summary

  • Angular momentum is the rotational analog of linear momentum.
  • It is conserved in isolated systems with no external torques.
  • Has practical and observable implications in sports, astronomy, and engineering.
  • Used to solve problems involving rotational motion and collisions.
