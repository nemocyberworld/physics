Elastic and Inelastic Collisions in One Dimension

Introduction

Collisions are events where two or more bodies come into contact with each other for a brief time and exert forces on each other. In physics, studying collisions helps us understand how momentum and energy are transferred during interactions.

Types of Collisions

1. Elastic Collision

In an elastic collision, both momentum and kinetic energy are conserved.

Examples:

  • Collisions between gas molecules
  • Collision between two steel balls
  • Newtonâ€™s cradle (a toy that demonstrates momentum conservation)

2. Inelastic Collision

In an inelastic collision, momentum is conserved, but kinetic energy is not. Some of the kinetic energy is converted into other forms of energy like heat, sound, or deformation energy.

Perfectly inelastic collision is a special case where the two bodies stick together after the collision.

Examples:

  • Car crashes
  • Clay balls sticking together
  • Football player tackles

Conservation Laws

1. Conservation of Momentum

In both types of collisions, the total momentum before and after collision remains the same.

$$ m_1u_1 + m_2u_2 = m_1v_1 + m_2v_2 $$

Where:

  • \( m_1, m_2 \) are the masses
  • \( u_1, u_2 \) are the initial velocities
  • \( v_1, v_2 \) are the final velocities

2. Conservation of Kinetic Energy (Elastic only)

$$ \frac{1}{2}m_1u_1^2 + \frac{1}{2}m_2u_2^2 = \frac{1}{2}m_1v_1^2 + \frac{1}{2}m_2v_2^2 $$

Real-World Examples

Elastic:

  • Billiard balls colliding
  • Atomic and subatomic particle collisions in accelerators
  • Ice skaters gliding into each other on a frictionless surface

Inelastic:

  • Train cars coupling together
  • A meteor impacting Earth
  • Boxers punching each other (body absorbs impact)

Example Problem: Elastic Collision

Problem: Two carts on a frictionless track collide elastically. Cart A has mass 2 kg and moves at 3 m/s. Cart B has mass 1 kg and is at rest. Find their velocities after the collision.

Solution:

\( m_1 = 2 \text{ kg}, \quad u_1 = 3 \text{ m/s} \)
\( m_2 = 1 \text{ kg}, \quad u_2 = 0 \)

Using the formulas for elastic collisions:

\( v_1 = \frac{(m_1 - m_2)u_1 + 2m_2u_2}{m_1 + m_2} = \frac{(2 - 1) \cdot 3 + 0}{3} = 1 \text{ m/s} \)
\( v_2 = \frac{(m_2 - m_1)u_2 + 2m_1u_1}{m_1 + m_2} = \frac{0 + 2 \cdot 2 \cdot 3}{3} = 4 \text{ m/s} \)

Answer: Cart A moves at 1 m/s, Cart B at 4 m/s after collision.

Example Problem: Perfectly Inelastic Collision

Problem: Two blocks stick together after colliding. Block A (2 kg) is moving at 4 m/s and block B (3 kg) is at rest. Find their final velocity after collision.

Solution:

\( v = \frac{m_1u_1 + m_2u_2}{m_1 + m_2} = \frac{2 \cdot 4 + 3 \cdot 0}{5} = \frac{8}{5} = 1.6 \text{ m/s} \)

Answer: They move together at 1.6 m/s after the collision.

Practical Applications

  • Car safety: Engineers use inelastic collision principles to design crumple zones that absorb energy.
  • Sports: Analysis of ball-bat collisions in baseball or tackles in football.
  • Space science: Spacecraft docking uses principles of inelastic collisions.
  • Particle physics: Colliders like the LHC use elastic/inelastic concepts to study fundamental particles.

Fun Fact!

The term "elastic" comes from elasticity in materials. Even when steel balls collide, they may sound like they're bouncing perfectly, but there's always a tiny energy loss in the form of sound or heat!
