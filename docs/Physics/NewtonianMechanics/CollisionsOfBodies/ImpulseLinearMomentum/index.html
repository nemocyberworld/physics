Impulse and Linear Momentum

Chapter: Collisions of Bodies

Understanding how objects behave when they collide is essential in many areas of physics, from car crashes to sports. Two key concepts involved in analyzing collisions are Impulse and Linear Momentum.

1. What is Linear Momentum?

Linear momentum is a measure of an object's motion. It depends on two things: the object's mass and its velocity. The greater the mass or speed of an object, the greater its momentum.

$$ \vec{p} = m \vec{v} $$

Where:

  • \( \vec{p} \) = momentum (in kg·m/s)
  • \( m \) = mass (in kg)
  • \( \vec{v} \) = velocity (in m/s)

2. What is Impulse?

Impulse is the effect of a force applied over a time interval. It tells us how much the momentum of an object has changed.

$$ \vec{J} = F \Delta t = \Delta \vec{p} $$

Where:

  • \( \vec{J} \) = impulse (in N·s or kg·m/s)
  • \( F \) = force (in N)
  • \( \Delta t \) = time interval (in s)
  • \( \Delta \vec{p} \) = change in momentum

3. Real World Examples

A. Car Crashes

When a car crashes, its momentum changes rapidly. Safety features like airbags and crumple zones increase the time over which the force acts, reducing the impulse force and minimizing injuries.

B. Sports

In baseball, when a bat hits a ball, the force over a short time (impulse) changes the ball’s momentum dramatically. A good hit transfers maximum momentum.

C. Jumping off a Boat

Jumping forward off a boat causes the boat to move backward. This is conservation of momentum: the total momentum before and after must be equal.

4. Conservation of Momentum

In the absence of external forces, the total linear momentum of a system remains constant. This principle is vital in understanding collisions.

$$ m_1 v_{1i} + m_2 v_{2i} = m_1 v_{1f} + m_2 v_{2f} $$

Where:

  • \( v_{1i}, v_{2i} \) = initial velocities
  • \( v_{1f}, v_{2f} \) = final velocities

5. Types of Collisions

There are two main types of collisions:

  • Elastic Collisions: Both momentum and kinetic energy are conserved. Example: collision of billiard balls.
  • Inelastic Collisions: Momentum is conserved, but kinetic energy is not. Example: car crashes.

6. Practical Applications

  • Crash Testing: Engineers use impulse and momentum to design safer vehicles.
  • Rocket Propulsion: Rockets expel gas backward to gain forward momentum.
  • Martial Arts: Momentum transfer is used to break boards or throw opponents.
  • Sports Training: Coaches analyze impulse to improve athletes’ performance in activities like sprinting or hitting a ball.

7. Worked Example

A 0.15 kg baseball moving at 40 m/s is hit by a bat and leaves at 50 m/s in the opposite direction. What impulse did the bat impart?

Solution:

\( \Delta p = m(v_f - v_i) = 0.15(-50 - 40) = 0.15(-90) = -13.5 \text{ kg·m/s} \)

So the impulse is 13.5 kg·m/s in the opposite direction.

8. Fun Fact!

The term "momentum" comes from Latin movimentum, meaning "movement." In space, astronauts use the principle of momentum to move themselves around by pushing off surfaces or throwing objects in the opposite direction!

9. Summary

  • Linear momentum is mass × velocity.
  • Impulse is force × time and equals change in momentum.
  • Momentum is conserved in all collisions.
  • Understanding these concepts helps in designing safer vehicles, improving athletic performance, and understanding physical interactions.
